Context

A planning process held together
by three platforms and manual effort.

The client's Advanced Analytics (AA) team was responsible for onboarding Distribution Centres (DCs) onto a workforce planning tool and providing ongoing planning support. These DCs received finished goods from vendors and managed the movement of merchandise through two broad flows: inbound — from receiving through decanting and putaway into inventory — and outbound — from replenishment through speedpack and manpack to outbound dispatch.

The existing solution depended on PowerBI, Excel, and Databricks operating in concert. Data lived across all three. Any structural change in one demanded synchronized updates across the others. Maintenance wasn't a background task; it was a constant, consuming responsibility.

The core tension
Every new DC required 2–3 months of custom dashboard work from the AA team. With DCs spread across vastly different demographics — some running permanent staff, others relying heavily on hiring agencies — the team had no clear path to a unified solution.

The client wanted two things: cut onboarding time from months to a week, and reduce the dependency of DC admins and managers on the AA team — for both initial setup and ongoing structural changes. I was the only designer on the engagement.

Three compounding problems
Fragmented data infrastructure
PowerBI, Databricks, and Excel each held a piece of the truth. Keeping them in sync was an ongoing operational burden — not a solvable problem within the existing architecture.
No scalable onboarding model
Each DC onboarding was effectively a bespoke project. Operational differences across geographies — workforce mix, shift structures, department configurations — meant no template survived contact with reality.
AA team as a permanent bottleneck
DC teams couldn't configure, modify, or adapt their own setups without AA involvement — pulling analytical capacity away from higher-value work and creating a dependency that compounded with every new DC.

Design Approach

Understanding the domain before
touching the interface.

Before any wireframe was drawn, I spent significant time understanding how retail DC operations actually work — the flow of merchandise from inbound gates through departments and out to fulfilment; the role distinctions between DC Admin, DC Planner, and DC Manager; and the planning rhythms each persona operated on. Very often, two of these roles were held by the same person, which shaped how the permission model needed to be structured.

Design conviction
"If you can describe a user's work back to them more precisely than they can describe it themselves, they'll trust you with everything else."

We built a detailed business process flow before our first client conversation — covering DC configuration, monthly modifications, planning cycles, and management review across all three personas. When we walked them through it, they had minor corrections, not fundamental ones. That moment set the tone for the entire engagement. The clients weren't evaluating us anymore; they were collaborating with us.

Business Process Flow High-level end-to-end DC lifecycle — covering DC configuration and setup, monthly modifications, planning cycles, and management review. Built through secondary research and validated with the client before interviews began.

To select interview participants, we ran a diversity assessment survey — gathering details about each DC's ways of working, workforce composition, and planning processes. This allowed us to select candidates whose approaches differed meaningfully from one another: different geographies, different workforce mixes, different planning styles. The result was a research base that surfaced genuine variation rather than confirming a single assumed norm.

Research Output
Key Insights from the Diversity Assessment Survey
DCs Surveyed
13 / 66
21 distinct departments identified — far exceeding initial assumptions
DCs categorise workforce by departments and department groups, with up to 21 unique departments including Inbound, Waving, VAS, MHE, and 3PL Management. This directly reshaped the department relationship screen layout.
Day–Department–Job Function–Shift is the universal planning cut
This four-level granularity was the most detailed planning structure that could accommodate all DCs surveyed — becoming the foundational filter hierarchy for the tool.
57% of DCs fulfil orders outside their own region
All 12 DCs handle international shipments. Regional and global demand fluctuations directly affect staffing needs — surfacing a design opportunity to notify planners of upcoming demand spikes.
Unplanned variables are the leading cause of plan modification
Unplanned leaves, maintenance shifts, machine breakdowns, and holiday seasons — alongside unpredictable weather and union strikes — are the primary triggers forcing DC teams to revise live plans.
Performance is tracked daily, weekly, and monthly across 5 core KPIs
Operator performance, packing rate, workload achieved %, utilisation rate, and throughput are the primary metrics. Daily review is most frequent; some DCs also track seasonal performance.
DC management makes five categories of high-stakes decisions
Resource allocation, hiring and layoff calls, staff upskilling, overtime planning, and site closure decisions all flow from plan performance data — directly defining what the DC Manager view needed to surface and prioritise.

Design Principles

Five decisions that shaped
how the platform behaves.

The diversity of DC operations — permanent vs agency workforce, varying shift structures, different department configurations — made a rigid template impossible. The design had to be configurable enough to accommodate genuine operational difference, while structured enough to remain trainable and maintainable without AA team support.

01
One tool that configures to any DC — not a custom build for each one
The old model required the AA team to build a bespoke tool for every DC, taking 2–3 months per deployment. The design challenge wasn't sequencing setup before planning — that always happened. It was building a single platform with enough structural flexibility that a DC Admin anywhere in the world could configure it to their own operational reality, without AA team involvement. Every design decision in the setup flow was in service of that: guided enough to be learnable, open enough to accommodate genuine difference.
Role-Based Navigation Two screens, two permission models. The DC Admin (back) has full access to DC Setup and configuration. The DC Planner (front) sees the same setup area as "DC Setup Preview" — read-only visibility with no edit access.
02
Build configurability into the structure, not the exceptions
Every DC runs differently. Some have three shifts; others two. Some departments are irrelevant for certain channels. Rather than hard-coding assumptions, the setup flow allowed admins to define department sequences, workforce thresholds, shift structures, and workload equations from scratch — within a guided framework. Configuration wasn't a workaround; it was the intended path.
Define Relationships DC Admin defines the department sequence — dragging departments from the list on the left into a visual flow that maps the order merchandise moves through the DC,
03
Let the forecast model carry the load; give planners authority over the exceptions
The system auto-generates workforce plans from demand forecasts across pre-defined scenarios. The Planner's role isn't to build plans from scratch; it's to review, adjust workload splits across shifts, and decide which scenario fits the coming week. This kept cognitive load appropriate: planners made judgement calls, not arithmetic ones.
Scenario Comparison Scenario comparison view — showing generated plans across multiple scenarios, with constrained and unconstrained views for each.
04
Progressive disclosure from channel to job function
Planning data is dense. A planner reviewing a week's workforce plan needs to move from day level through departments, job function, shifts without losing orientation. The interface was structured to reveal detail progressively on demand, not expose everything at once. The DC Manager persona needed even less depth: actuals vs forecast, cost performance, plan adherence.
Progressive Disclosure — Workforce View The planning table expands from day level down to departments and shifts — resource count and workload achieved visible at every level. A planner can stay at the day summary or drill into a specific department's shift breakdown without losing their place in the overall plan.
05
Design for global diversity without sacrificing operational coherence
DCs in different geographies operated with fundamentally different workforce models. A DC in China running via hiring agencies with a high proportion of temporary staff could not share a rigid template with a DC in Europe running predominantly permanent workers. The diversity assessment survey surfaced this variation deliberately — so the design could absorb it structurally rather than treating it as an edge case. One platform; every DC configuration it needed to be.
The Shift

From months of custom work
to one week, any DC.

The before/after wasn't a UX improvement story — it was an operational transformation. The platform moved ownership from the AA team to the DC teams, from bespoke builds to configurable structures, and from reactive maintenance to predictable, self-sufficient operations.

Before After
2–3 months to onboard each new DC, requiring full AA team involvement throughout → 1-week onboarding, configured independently by the DC Admin
Data split across PowerBI, Databricks, and Excel — requiring constant synchronization → Single unified platform; one source of truth for planning and actuals
Custom dashboards per DC, built from scratch for every demographic variation → Configurable structure that adapts to any DC's workforce model and department setup
Planners dependent on AA team for structural changes, configuration updates, and planning support → DC teams own their configuration and planning cycle end-to-end
No systematic scenario comparison — decisions based on experience and manual calculation → Auto-generated plans across multiple scenarios with constrained and unconstrained views for informed finalisation

What this project taught me about designing for operational diversity.
The instinct on a project like this is to find the common denominator — to build the version that works everywhere by asking nothing of the differences underneath. That instinct produces tools that technically function globally and practically serve no one. Real configurability isn't about stripping things down; it's about building a structure robust enough to hold genuinely different ways of working without breaking.

The diversity assessment survey was the single most consequential design decision I made on this project. It shaped who we talked to, what we heard, and ultimately what we built. Designing for one DC well would have produced a tool that worked for one DC. Designing for the full range of variation produced a platform.

I also learned that domain fluency isn't a nice-to-have in enterprise UX — it's the entry fee. Walking into client conversations already knowing the difference between constrained and unconstrained planning, or why shift-level workload splitting matters, changed the quality of every conversation we had from that point forward.
